# Pricing Strategies Reference

KOINO Capital Original IP. Price for value, not hours.

---

## Core Pricing Principles

### 1. Price on Value, Not Time
```
Wrong: "I charge $150/hour"
Right: "The investment is $15,000 for a system that will generate $150K+ in pipeline"
```

The client doesn't care how many hours it takes. They care what they get. Anchor to the outcome, not the input.

### 2. The 10x Rule
Your price should be no more than 1/10th of the value you create. If you can demonstrably generate $100K in value, charge $10K. The client gets a 10x return and you get paid well. Everyone wins.

### 3. Never Discount — Adjust Scope
When a prospect says "that's too expensive," never lower your price. Instead:
- Remove deliverables to hit their budget
- Phase the engagement (start smaller, expand on results)
- Change the payment terms (monthly vs. upfront)

Discounting trains clients to negotiate harder next time and devalues your work.

## Pricing Models for Service Businesses

### Model 1: Project-Based (Best for Defined Scope)
```
Deliverable: Complete sales funnel build
Price: $8,000
Timeline: 30 days
```
**Pros:** Clear expectations, easy to sell, clean scope
**Cons:** Scope creep risk, one-time revenue
**Best for:** Website builds, campaign launches, system implementations

### Model 2: Retainer (Best for Ongoing Services)
```
Service: Content marketing + social management
Price: $3,000/month
Commitment: 3-month minimum
```
**Pros:** Predictable recurring revenue, deep client relationship
**Cons:** Client expectations inflate over time, can feel like a job
**Best for:** Marketing, management, advisory, ongoing optimization

### Model 3: Performance-Based (Best for High-Confidence Delivery)
```
Base: $2,000/month
Performance bonus: 10% of attributed revenue above baseline
```
**Pros:** Aligns incentives, easy to justify value, uncapped upside
**Cons:** Requires tracking/attribution, delayed payment, risk
**Best for:** Lead gen, sales, advertising, conversion optimization

### Model 4: Productized Service (Best for Scale)
```
Tier 1 — Starter: $997/mo (5 blog posts, basic SEO)
Tier 2 — Growth: $2,497/mo (10 posts, full SEO, social distribution)
Tier 3 — Scale: $4,997/mo (20 posts, SEO, social, paid amplification, reporting)
```
**Pros:** Easy to sell, easy to deliver, scalable, clients self-select
**Cons:** Less customization, commoditization risk
**Best for:** Any repeatable service with clear deliverables

### Model 5: Value-Based (Best for High-Impact Engagements)
```
Discovery: What is this problem costing you? → $500K/year in lost revenue
Solution value: Recover 50% = $250K
Price: $25K (10x ROI)
```
**Pros:** Highest margins, client sees clear ROI, premium positioning
**Cons:** Requires strong discovery, harder to sell to unsophisticated buyers
**Best for:** Consulting, strategy, transformation projects

## Pricing Psychology Tactics

### Anchoring
Always present the highest option first. The prospect's brain anchors to the first number, making everything after feel reasonable.

```
Option 3 (Premium): $10,000
Option 2 (Standard): $6,000  ← most will choose this
Option 1 (Basic): $3,000
```

### Decoy Pricing
Make one option clearly inferior to nudge toward your preferred option:

```
Basic: $2,000 (5 deliverables)
Standard: $3,500 (12 deliverables)  ← 2.4x value for 1.75x price
Premium: $5,000 (15 deliverables)
```

Standard looks like the obvious smart choice. That's the point.

### Price Bracketing
When asked "what does this cost?" before discovery, give a range:

"For a company your size with this type of challenge, our clients typically invest between $5,000 and $15,000 depending on scope. Once I understand your situation better, I can give you a precise number."

This does three things:
1. Filters out people who can't afford you
2. Anchors to the high end
3. Buys you time for proper discovery

### Annual Discount Framing
```
Monthly: $500/month
Annual: $5,000/year (save $1,000 — get 2 months free)
```
Frame the discount as "months free" not "percentage off." "2 months free" feels bigger than "17% off."

## When to Raise Prices

Raise prices when any of these are true:
- You're closing >70% of proposals (you're too cheap)
- You have a waitlist
- Your results consistently exceed client expectations
- You've added new capabilities or team members
- Your case studies show clear, quantified ROI
- You haven't raised prices in 6+ months

**How to raise prices:**
- New clients get new pricing immediately
- Existing clients get 60-day notice with grandfathering for 1 renewal cycle
- Never apologize for raising prices — frame it as investment in better delivery

## Pricing Red Flags

- Client asks for your hourly rate before understanding the scope
- Client wants to pay "when they see results" (they won't attribute results to you)
- Client compares you to Fiverr or Upwork freelancers
- Client asks for a "test project" at reduced rate with promise of "more work later"
- Client's budget is <50% of your minimum (no amount of negotiation will close this)
- Client wants unlimited revisions (scope will expand forever)
