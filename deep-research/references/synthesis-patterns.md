# Synthesis Patterns — Extracting Insight from Data

## The Synthesis Gap

The difference between research and intelligence is synthesis. Research collects facts. Intelligence produces insight. This document covers the patterns for crossing that gap.

## Pattern 1: Convergence Mapping

**When to use**: You have 10+ sources and need to find the signal.

1. List every distinct claim or finding
2. Group claims by theme
3. For each theme, count how many independent sources support it
4. The themes with highest independent convergence are your highest-confidence findings
5. The themes with LOW convergence but HIGH source quality are your most interesting findings
6. Claims that appear in only one source are either noise or alpha — investigate further

## Pattern 2: Tension Mapping

**When to use**: Sources disagree and you need to understand why.

1. Identify the specific point of disagreement
2. For each side: who says this, what evidence do they cite, what are their incentives?
3. Check if the disagreement is factual (different data), methodological (different analysis), or ideological (different values)
4. Factual disagreements can often be resolved by finding the primary data
5. Methodological disagreements reveal the most about the topic's complexity
6. Ideological disagreements tell you about the stakeholder landscape
7. **The tension itself is a finding** — report it, don't resolve it artificially

## Pattern 3: Absence Analysis

**When to use**: Always. The most important skill in research.

Ask:
- What SHOULD exist but doesn't? (missing data, missing competitors, missing regulation)
- Who ISN'T talking about this who should be? (silent stakeholders)
- What time period has NO coverage? (information gaps)
- What obvious question has NO answer in any source? (unasked questions)
- What data would PROVE the thesis but hasn't been collected? (measurement gaps)

Absences are often more informative than presences. A company that stops reporting a metric is telling you something. A market with no negative reviews is suspicious. A topic with no academic research may be too new, too niche, or too suppressed.

## Pattern 4: Temporal Decomposition

**When to use**: Understanding how something evolved reveals where it's going.

1. Build a timeline of key events, statements, and data points
2. Identify inflection points: when did the narrative change? What caused it?
3. Look for ACCELERATION or DECELERATION patterns
4. Compare the current narrative to the narrative 1, 2, 5 years ago
5. Identify what was PREDICTED but didn't happen (failed predictions reveal assumptions)
6. Identify what HAPPENED that nobody predicted (surprises reveal blind spots)

## Pattern 5: Incentive Mapping

**When to use**: Understanding people, organizations, or markets.

For each key player:
1. How do they make money? (revenue model)
2. What would they lose if [X] happened? (risk exposure)
3. What are they optimizing for? (stated vs revealed preferences)
4. Who do they report to? (accountability structure)
5. What have they DONE vs what have they SAID? (actions > words)
6. Where do incentives ALIGN across players? (cooperation zones)
7. Where do incentives CONFLICT? (competition/friction zones)

The biggest research breakthroughs come from identifying misaligned incentives that others assume are aligned.

## Pattern 6: Analogy Mining

**When to use**: Researching something new or unprecedented.

1. Identify the closest historical analogies
2. Map the similarities AND differences explicitly
3. What happened in the analogous cases? (outcomes)
4. What was different about cases that succeeded vs failed?
5. Which differences between the analogy and current situation are MATERIAL?
6. **Warning**: Analogies are powerful but dangerous. They can blind you to genuine novelty. Always ask: "In what way is this situation genuinely unprecedented?"

## Pattern 7: Layer Peeling

**When to use**: Topic seems simple on the surface.

1. **Layer 1**: What does a Google search tell you? (public narrative)
2. **Layer 2**: What do practitioners say? (forums, Slack communities, Reddit)
3. **Layer 3**: What do the data/numbers say? (filings, datasets, metrics)
4. **Layer 4**: What do insiders say privately? (Glassdoor, anonymous forums, off-record)
5. **Layer 5**: What do the incentives and structures say? (follow the money)
6. **Layer 6**: What does the absence of information say? (what's missing?)

Each layer peeled reveals a more accurate picture. Most "research" stops at Layer 1-2.

## Pattern 8: So What Extraction

**When to use**: Always, as the final synthesis step.

For every finding, ask three questions in sequence:
1. **"So what?"** — Why does this matter? (implication)
2. **"To whom?"** — Who should care about this? (stakeholder)
3. **"By when?"** — What's the time horizon for action? (urgency)

Then:
4. **"What would I bet on?"** — If forced to make a decision based on this research, what would it be? (conviction test)
5. **"What would change my mind?"** — What new information would reverse my conclusion? (falsifiability)
6. **"What's the minimum viable action?"** — What's the smallest thing someone could do RIGHT NOW based on this? (activation energy)

## Meta-Pattern: The Research Stack

Layer these patterns for maximum insight:

```
Convergence Mapping  →  What do we know?
Tension Mapping      →  Where do sources disagree?
Absence Analysis     →  What don't we know?
Temporal Decomp      →  How did we get here?
Incentive Mapping    →  Why do people act this way?
Analogy Mining       →  What can history teach us?
Layer Peeling        →  What's under the surface?
So What Extraction   →  What should we do?
```

Running all 8 patterns on a single topic produces institutional-grade intelligence that is genuinely better than what most people can produce — not because the data is secret, but because the SYNTHESIS is rigorous.
