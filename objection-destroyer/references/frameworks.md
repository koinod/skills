# Objection Handling Frameworks

Seven battle-tested frameworks. Each with the psychology behind it, when to use it, and real examples across industries.

---

## 1. Feel-Felt-Found

**Origin**: Traditional consultative selling, refined by Tom Hopkins.
**Psychology**: Cialdini's Social Proof + Kahneman's System 1 pattern matching. People trust that others like them have navigated the same uncertainty.

**Structure**:
1. "I understand how you **feel**..."  (validate)
2. "Others in your position have **felt** the same way..."  (normalize)
3. "What they **found** was..."  (reframe with outcome)

**When to use**: Trust objections, price objections from amiable buyers, any objection with emotional charge.

**When NOT to use**: With analytical buyers who see it as scripted. With drivers who find it patronizing.

### Examples

**Insurance -- "I don't trust insurance companies"**
> "I completely understand how you feel -- a lot of my clients felt the exact same way before we worked together. They'd been burned by fine print and surprise exclusions. What they found was that when someone actually sits down and walks through every line of the policy with them -- no hidden anything -- it changes the whole experience. That's exactly what I do. Can I show you?"

**SaaS -- "We tried something similar and it didn't work"**
> "I hear you, and I understand that feeling. Several of our best customers felt the same way -- they'd been burned by [competitor] and were skeptical. What they found was that the difference came down to implementation support. We don't just hand you software and disappear. Our onboarding team stays with you for 90 days. Want me to connect you with [similar company] who was in your exact position six months ago?"

**D2D -- "I'm not interested" (at the door)**
> "Totally get it -- most people feel that way when someone shows up at their door. The neighbors I just spoke with at [address] felt the same way. What they found was that a 2-minute look at their current bill saved them $40 a month. Mind if I take 2 minutes?"

## 2. Isolate & Solve

**Origin**: Sandler Selling System's Pain Funnel technique.
**Psychology**: Commitment & Consistency (Cialdini). Once they confirm the objection is the ONLY obstacle, agreeing to buy after it's resolved becomes psychologically consistent.

**Structure**:
1. "So if I'm hearing you right, [objection] is your concern?"  (reflect)
2. "Beyond that, is there anything else holding you back?"  (isolate)
3. "So if we can solve [objection], you'd be ready to move forward?"  (conditional close)
4. [Solve the objection]
5. "We've addressed that -- shall we get started?"  (close)

**When to use**: Authority objections (smokescreen detection), timing objections, any situation where you suspect the stated objection isn't the real one.

**When NOT to use**: When the prospect is clearly emotional or frustrated. Feels too logical/transactional in those moments.

### Examples

**Agency -- "I need to talk to my partner"**
> "Absolutely, that makes sense. Let me ask -- beyond getting your partner's input, is there anything else that would prevent you from moving forward?"
> [If no]: "Great. So if your partner is on board, we're good to go? ... Perfect. What if we set up a quick 15-minute call with all three of us? I can answer any questions they have directly. Does tomorrow at 2 work?"

**SaaS -- "We need to wait until next quarter"**
> "I understand planning cycles. Beyond the timing, is the solution itself what you're looking for?"
> [If yes]: "Got it. So functionally we're aligned -- it's just about when. Here's what I'd suggest: we lock in this quarter's pricing now, and we start implementation whenever you're ready. That way you don't lose the rate and you don't feel rushed. Fair?"

**Real Estate -- "The price is too high"**
> "I hear you on the price. Beyond the asking price, do you love the property?"
> [If yes]: "So the house is right, the location is right, the timing is right -- it's just the number. Let me show you what comparable properties sold for in the last 90 days, and then let's talk about what an offer strategy looks like."

## 3. Boomerang

**Origin**: Jordan Belfort's "Straight Line Selling" (looping technique).
**Psychology**: Reframing (Kahneman). Takes the objection and uses its own energy to create a reason to buy. Cognitive judo.

**Structure**:
1. Acknowledge the objection
2. "That's exactly why..."  (turn it into a benefit)
3. Reinforce with evidence
4. Close

**When to use**: Timing objections, price objections where value is high, competition objections. Works best with drivers and expressives.

**When NOT to use**: When the objection is deeply personal or emotional. Feels dismissive.

### Examples

**SaaS -- "We're happy with our current solution"**
> "That's actually exactly why we should talk. Companies that are 'happy' with their current tool are usually the ones leaving the most money on the table -- because they've gotten comfortable and stopped benchmarking. Our best clients came from [competitor] thinking everything was fine. Within 30 days they found 15-20% efficiency gains they didn't know they were missing. Can I show you the gap analysis we ran for [similar company]?"

**Insurance -- "I already have coverage"**
> "That's exactly why this conversation matters. Most people who have coverage haven't reviewed it in 2-3 years -- and in that time, rates drop, coverage gaps appear, and life changes happen. The fact that you already have coverage means you already understand the value of protection. I'm just here to make sure your protection actually matches where you are today. Can I run a free comparison? Takes 10 minutes."

**D2D Solar -- "I can't afford it right now"**
> "That's exactly why this makes sense right now. You're paying $200/month to the power company with zero return. Our program replaces that bill with a lower payment AND builds equity. You literally can't afford NOT to switch. Let me show you the numbers."

## 4. Preemptive Strike

**Origin**: Chris Voss's "accusation audit" from Never Split the Difference.
**Psychology**: Inoculation Theory (McGuire). By raising the objection yourself first, you defuse it. The prospect feels understood and can't use it as an escape hatch.

**Structure**:
1. Before they object, raise the concern yourself
2. "You're probably thinking..."  or  "Most people at this point wonder..."
3. Address it head-on
4. Continue pitch with the objection neutralized

**When to use**: In presentations, pitches, demos. When you KNOW which objections are coming based on your industry data.

**When NOT to use**: Don't preempt objections they weren't going to raise -- you'll plant seeds of doubt.

### Examples

**Agency Pitch ($5K/mo retainer)**
> "Before I show you the investment, let me address what you're probably already thinking: '$5K a month is a lot.' You're right. It is. And there's a reason we don't compete on price. Our last three clients in your space saw an average 340% ROI within 90 days. I'd rather you pay us $5K and make $17K than pay someone else $2K and make nothing. Let me show you the math."

**Startup to Investor -- "It's too early"**
> "I know what you might be thinking -- 'This is early-stage, it's risky.' You're right. It is early. That's exactly why the upside is what it is. The companies in your portfolio that returned 50x weren't Series B safe bets. They were early-stage conviction plays. Here's why this is one of those."

**Recruiting -- Salary Negotiation**
> "I know the number I'm about to share might be higher than your initial range. Before I do, let me explain why it reflects the market and the value I'll deliver in the first 90 days."

## 5. Social Proof Stack

**Origin**: Cialdini's Social Proof principle, amplified.
**Psychology**: Herd behavior + similarity bias. People don't just want to know others bought -- they want to know someone LIKE THEM bought, in a SIMILAR situation, and got a POSITIVE outcome.

**Structure**:
1. **Similar Person**: "We worked with [name/company] who is very similar to you..."
2. **Similar Situation**: "They were in the same position -- [describe matching circumstance]..."
3. **Positive Outcome**: "And within [timeframe], they [specific result]..."
4. **Bridge**: "I think the same thing could happen for you. Here's why..."

**When to use**: Need objections, trust objections, competition objections. Devastating when you have real case studies.

**When NOT to use**: When you don't have a genuinely similar reference. Fabricated social proof destroys trust permanently.

### Examples

**SaaS -- "We don't need this right now"**
> "I get it. [Company X] -- they're in [same industry], similar size, similar tech stack -- said the exact same thing 8 months ago. They were doing fine. But when they ran our audit, they found they were spending 12 hours a week on manual processes they didn't even realize were manual. They signed up, and within 60 days, they'd freed up their ops team to focus on growth. They grew 22% last quarter. Want me to connect you with their VP of Ops?"

**Insurance -- "I'm too young for life insurance"**
> "I hear that a lot from people your age. I had a client, 28, just got married, felt the same way. He said, 'I'm healthy, I don't need this.' Six months later, he was diagnosed with a condition that would have made him uninsurable. Because we'd already locked in his rate, he's covered for life at the lowest premium he'll ever get. The best time to buy is when you don't need it."

## 6. Cost of Inaction

**Origin**: Kahneman & Tversky's Loss Aversion research. Losses hurt 2x more than equivalent gains feel good.
**Psychology**: Frame the conversation not as "cost of buying" but "cost of NOT buying." Shift from gain frame to loss frame.

**Structure**:
1. Acknowledge the price/timing concern
2. "Let me ask you this -- what's it costing you right now to NOT have this solved?"
3. Quantify the cost of inaction (dollars, time, risk, opportunity)
4. Compare: cost of inaction vs. cost of solution
5. "So the question isn't whether you can afford this. It's whether you can afford not to have it."

**When to use**: Price objections (especially from analytical buyers), timing/delay objections. Works when you can quantify the gap.

**When NOT to use**: When the cost of inaction is genuinely low. Forcing this framework on a non-urgent problem feels manipulative.

### Examples

**SaaS -- "It's too expensive at $2K/month"**
> "I understand the concern. Let me ask -- you mentioned your team spends about 15 hours a week on manual reporting. At an average loaded cost of $50/hour, that's $3,000 a month in labor on something our tool automates completely. So the question isn't whether $2K/month is expensive. It's whether you want to keep paying $3K/month for a worse result. The tool pays for itself and gives you $1K/month back."

**Insurance -- "I'll think about it and get back to you"**
> "Totally fair. But let me share something: your rate is based on your health and age today. Every year you wait, the premium goes up an average of 8-10%. And if anything changes health-wise between now and when you decide, you could be denied entirely. The cost of waiting isn't zero -- it's a guaranteed price increase. Locking in today's rate is the cheapest this will ever be."

**Agency -- "We can handle marketing in-house"**
> "You absolutely could. But let's look at what that actually costs: a full-time marketing hire is $60-80K plus benefits. You'd need tools -- another $500-1,000/month. And the ramp time is 3-6 months before they're producing results. Our retainer gets you a full team, all tools included, producing results in 30 days, for less than half the cost of one hire. In-house isn't cheaper -- it's just slower."

## 7. The Takeaway

**Origin**: Sandler's "Negative Reverse Selling." Elements in Belfort and Voss.
**Psychology**: Scarcity (Cialdini) + Reactance Theory (Brehm). When something is taken away, people want it more. Strategic withdrawal creates urgency and rebalances power.

**Structure**:
1. Sense pull-back signals or repeated stalling
2. Withdraw the offer or reduce your enthusiasm
3. "Maybe this isn't the right fit for you, and that's okay."
4. Pause. Let silence work.
5. If they re-engage, they've shifted from resistant to pursuing.

**When to use**: When the prospect is stalling repeatedly, when they have all the information and still won't commit, when you've handled the same objection twice. Also powerful in negotiations.

**When NOT to use**: On the first objection. It's a later-stage play. Using it too early makes you look disinterested or desperate.

### Examples

**Any industry -- Repeated stalling**
> "You know what, I want to be respectful of your time. It sounds like this might not be the right fit right now, and I'd rather be honest about that than keep pushing. If things change, you have my number. But I should let you know -- this pricing is only available through [date], and I've got two other companies in your space I'm talking to this week. No pressure either way."

**Agency -- "Let me think about it" (third time)**
> "Honestly, I think you should take your time. This is a big decision and I don't want you to feel rushed. That said, I do have limited bandwidth -- I can only take on two new clients this quarter, and I'm in conversations with others. So if you do decide you want to move forward, just know that the sooner we lock it in, the sooner I can guarantee your spot. If not, totally fine -- I'll keep the door open."

**Negotiation -- Salary**
> "I appreciate the offer, and I've really enjoyed the process. That said, the number is below what I'm seeing in the market for this role, and I want to make sure this works for both of us. If the budget truly can't flex, I completely understand -- no hard feelings. But I'd be leaving value on the table, and I don't want to start a role feeling like I compromised. Is there room to revisit this?"
